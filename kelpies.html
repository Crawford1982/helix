What Are The Kelpies?

The Kelpies are two enormous horse-head sculptures standing 30 metres tall at the eastern entrance to the Forth and Clyde Canal at Helix Park in Falkirk. Designed by Scottish sculptor Andy Scott and unveiled in April 2014, they're the largest equine sculptures in the world — each one weighing over 300 tonnes and built from stainless steel.

The sculptures are a tribute to the heavy horses that once powered Scotland's canals and industries. The name comes from the shape-shifting water spirits of Scottish mythology — supernatural horses said to inhabit the lochs and rivers of Scotland.

The Story Behind the Sculptures

The Kelpies were commissioned as part of a major regeneration project to transform 350 hectares of land between Falkirk and Grangemouth into the parkland that is now the Helix. Andy Scott worked on the design over several years, with the horses modelled on real Clydesdales called Duke and Baron.

Construction took around 90 days with a team of specialist steelworkers. The sculptures are made from structural steel covered in stainless steel cladding — thousands of individual plates bolted together to create the distinctive angular surface you see today.

The project cost around £5 million and was funded by the National Lottery through the Big Lottery Fund, alongside contributions from Falkirk Council and other partners.

What to Expect When You Visit

The Kelpies sit at the eastern end of the Forth and Clyde Canal in a specially designed plaza with a reflection pool in front. On a still day, the reflections in the water are spectacular — and that's before the lights come on at dusk.

You can walk right up to the base of the sculptures for free, any time of day or night. There's no fence or barrier — you can get close enough to appreciate the detail of the steel plating and the sheer scale of them.

If you want to go inside, you'll need to book a guided tour through the visitor centre. The 25-minute tour takes you beneath and inside one of the horse heads, where guides explain the engineering and history. It's well worth doing.

The Kelpies at Night

The sculptures are internally lit from dusk until dawn, changing colour roughly every 30 minutes. They're arguably even more impressive at night than during the day — the lighting transforms them completely. The reflection pool in front makes for stunning photographs.

Night visits are free. Use the Helix Car Park (free, open 24/7) as the Kelpies Car Park locks in the evening.

What Are Kelpies in Scottish Mythology?

In Scottish folklore, kelpies are shape-shifting water spirits that most commonly appear as horses. They were said to inhabit the lochs, rivers and streams of Scotland, luring travellers to ride on their backs before dragging them into the water. The myth served as a warning — particularly to children — to stay away from dangerous waterways.

Andy Scott chose the name and the horse form to connect the sculptures to both Scottish mythology and the real heavy horses that were central to Scotland's industrial canal network. The horses that once pulled barges along the Forth and Clyde Canal were essential to trade and transport — and The Kelpies stand as a monument to their contribution.
